# Brief: Azulito landing page

## What Azulito is

A personal lab. A container for experiments—some serious (a startup called Telltale is the current one), some playful, some half-formed. It's a place where I put things I'm thinking about. It's not a studio, not an agency, not labs. It's a one-word idea: Azulito—"little blue one" in Spanish. Warm, curious, unhurried.

The domain is `azulito.co.nz`.

## What the landing page needs to do

Almost nothing. That's the point.

It should make the visitor pause. Most websites in 2026 shout—hero text, value prop, three-column features, CTA, testimonials, footer. This shouldn't. The negative space is the message: *this is a place where someone thinks, not a funnel.*

When you land on `azulito.co.nz`, the first impression should be emptiness. Air. Room to breathe. Then—only if you look—there's something small tucked into the bottom right corner. That's where the content lives. Name, maybe a one-line description of what Azulito is, a small list of experiments (currently just Telltale), a way to reach me. Bury it. Let the visitor discover it rather than have it shoved at them.

## The anti-AI-design brief

This is the core constraint. Most sites designed by or with AI tools right now share a visual grammar: centred hero, gradient backgrounds, rounded-corner cards, "Built with [stack]" badge, three feature columns with icons, dark mode toggle in the top right, a CTA button that says "Get started" or "Learn more." It's competent and completely forgettable.

I want Azulito to break that template on purpose. Not by being weird for weird's sake, but by being quiet and specific in a way no template generates. Specific ways to do this:

- **Composition**: don't centre everything. Off-centre, bottom-right, asymmetric. The whole page can be 90% empty.
- **No hero section**. No big headline. No subhead. No CTA button.
- **No icons**. No illustrations. No stock-feeling anything.
- **No dark/light toggle**. Pick one and commit.
- **No "Built with" badge, no "Made by AI" aesthetic cues**.
- **Details over decoration**. A well-set line of type does more than a gradient.

The test: if the page could plausibly exist on a personal site from 2005 or a gallery catalogue from 1978, we're probably on the right track. If it looks like it could be a Vercel starter template, start over.

## Typography

Type is doing most of the work here, so this is where to spend the care. I've been reading *Just My Type* by Simon Garfield, which frames typefaces as having personalities, histories, and moods—and that's how I want you to approach this. A few directions worth considering:

- **Serif for warmth and intention**. A well-drawn book-weight serif (something in the lineage of Garamond, Sabon, or more contemporary ones like Söhne Breit or GT Super) signals "someone chose this." It reads as considered, not defaulted. It feels like a person wrote it, not a product page.
- **Avoid the default sans stack**. Inter, Geist, SF Pro, Helvetica Now—these are the visual equivalent of saying "hello, I used Figma's default." They're fine typefaces but they're the wallpaper of 2026 SaaS. If you go sans, go somewhere more specific: a Grotesque with character (GT America, ABC Diatype, Söhne), or a humanist with warmth.
- **One typeface, maybe two**. Not three. Restraint is the whole brief.
- **Type size matters more than colour**. Small, carefully set type in the corner will feel more intentional than anything bigger. Think of how a gallery labels a painting—14px, generous leading, left-aligned, no fuss.

Feel free to push back or suggest something I haven't considered. The Garfield reference is about the *attitude* toward type (that it matters, that it carries meaning, that defaults are a choice not an escape), not a prescription for a specific font.

## What I want to see

A first pass at:
1. The landing state—what someone sees in the first second
2. What's in the bottom-right corner and how it's arranged
3. Font choice with a sentence on why

Not a full design system. Not mobile breakpoints yet. Just the feel.

## Stuff I don't want

- Gradients
- Glassmorphism
- Rounded-corner cards
- Emojis
- A hero section
- Any CTA
- The word "Welcome"
- A blue that looks like Facebook blue (if we use blue at all—azulito means "little blue"—make it specific: a faded cyanotype blue, a Klein blue, a washed-out sky, something with a reference)
- Anything that feels like it came out of v0 or Lovable

## Tone reference

Think: the back of a good paperback. A museum wall label. A Japanese business card. A hand-set letterpress invitation. Quiet, specific, confident enough not to shout.
